# R2a Project Roast & Venture Stress-Test Methodology

## Question

How can K R&D Lab review early-stage projects in a way that is sharp, useful, ethical, and commercially actionable without reducing the work to casual criticism?

## Scope

- `R2b` public project roast case
- `R2c` founder-facing landing-page roast
- `R2d` social-impact and adoption-risk roast

## What a project roast is

A project roast is a structured stress-test of a product, startup, civic project, research product, or public-facing initiative.

It is not a takedown.

It is a practical diagnostic that asks:

- What is the project trying to make possible?
- Who needs it badly enough to care?
- What does a cold outsider understand in the first 10-30 seconds?
- Where does the idea over-explain, under-prove, or hide the useful part?
- What would make the project easier to adopt, fund, pilot, or trust?
- What social or ecosystem effect could the project create if it works?

## Boundary from a resonance review

This methodology is for critique plus recommendations.

If the goal is to capture personal impressions, associations, taste, discomfort, belief, or "do I see a future here?" without producing an action plan, use `R2c Project Resonance Review Methodology` instead.

In short:

- `project roast` = diagnostic critique plus recommendations
- `resonance review` = argued impression plus perspective verdict

## Core lenses

| Lens | Main question | Typical output |
|---|---|---|
| Commercial viability | Who would pay, pilot, fund, or adopt this? | ICP, pilot path, pricing or funding hypothesis |
| Cold-user usability | Does a new visitor understand the value fast enough? | Landing-page, demo, onboarding, messaging fixes |
| Trust and proof | What makes the claim believable? | Evidence gaps, validation path, credibility assets |
| Social impact | What changes if this project is adopted? | beneficiary map, risk map, public-value hypothesis |
| Operational feasibility | Can the team actually deliver the next useful version? | MVP scope, sequencing, resourcing risks |
| Narrative clarity | Is the story too abstract, too technical, or too vague? | rewritten hero, positioning map, clearer user journey |

## Scoring model

The methodology can use two independent scores:

1. **Commercial readiness score**
   - ability to become a pilot, product, grant case, paid service, or strategic partnership

2. **Cold-user readiness score**
   - ability to make a new visitor understand, trust, and try the project quickly

The point is not to shame the project with a number.

The point is to expose where the next improvement should happen first.

## Recommended score interpretation

| Score | Meaning |
|---:|---|
| 0-2 | Not legible yet |
| 3-4 | Interesting but hard to enter |
| 5-6 | Has a real core, but needs sharper proof or packaging |
| 7-8 | Strong enough for pilot conversations |
| 9-10 | Highly legible, testable, and adoption-ready |

## Standard roast structure

1. Project snapshot
2. What is already strong
3. Main adoption blockers
4. Commercial readiness score
5. Cold-user readiness score
6. Trust and proof gaps
7. Social-impact or ecosystem-impact hypothesis
8. Recommended positioning
9. Recommended landing/demo changes
10. 7-day, 30-day, and 90-day action plan

## Why social impact belongs here

Some projects are not only product ideas. They can affect communities, governance, education, health, civic trust, labor, ecology, or access to opportunity.

For those projects, a useful roast should ask not only:

```text
How can this convert better?
```

but also:

```text
What changes in the world if this actually works?
Who benefits?
Who could be harmed or excluded?
What would responsible adoption look like?
```

This makes the method useful for venture evaluation, grant positioning, public-interest design, and founder strategy.

## Publication rule

Only publish a full named-project roast when one of the following is true:

- the project owner explicitly agrees to public publication
- the analysis uses only public material and avoids private conversation details
- the output is anonymized enough that it becomes a reusable methodological example

Otherwise, keep the detailed roast private and publish only the methodology or an anonymized case pattern.
